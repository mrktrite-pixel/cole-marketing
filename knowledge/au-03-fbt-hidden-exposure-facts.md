## The Rule

**Name:** Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT) — Non-Cash Benefit Trap for Small Business Owners
**Short form:** Any non-cash benefit you provide to yourself, your family, or any employee through your private company is potentially subject to FBT at 47% on a grossed-up basis. The FBT year runs 1 April to 31 March (different from income tax year). Common traps: company car private use, paying personal expenses through the company, providing meal entertainment, gym memberships, school fees.
**Operative law:** Fringe Benefits Tax Assessment Act 1986 (FBTAA 1986)
- s.5B — FBT rate (47% for 2025-26)
- s.9 — Statutory formula method for car benefits
- s.10 — Operating cost (logbook) method for car benefits
- s.58P — Minor benefits exemption (under $300, infrequent + irregular)
- s.58Y — Otherwise deductible rule
- s.135C — Reportable Fringe Benefits Amount (RFBA) threshold $2,000+
**ATO landing page:** https://www.ato.gov.au/businesses-and-organisations/hiring-and-paying-your-workers/fringe-benefits-tax

## The Threshold

**Effective date:** FBT regime commenced 1 July 1986 (FBTAA 1986 enactment).
**FBT rate:** **47%** for 2025-26 (set at top marginal income tax rate plus 2% Medicare levy).
**Type 1 gross-up rate:** **2.0802** (where employer can claim GST input tax credit)
**Type 2 gross-up rate:** **1.8868** (where no GST credit available — GST-free or input taxed)
**Statutory car formula:** 20% of GST-inclusive base value (flat rate since 1 April 2014, replaced sliding kilometre scale).
**FBT benchmark interest rate (deemed interest in operating cost method):** 8.77% for 2025-26 (TD 2025/3) — note this is DIFFERENT from Division 7A benchmark rate of 8.37%.
**Operating cost depreciation rate:** 25% diminishing value (for cars purchased on or after 10 May 2006).
**Minor benefits exemption threshold:** $300 per benefit, provided infrequently and irregularly.
**RFBA reporting threshold:** Grossed-up taxable value of $2,000+ per employee → must be reported on payment summary.
**FBT year:** 1 April to 31 March (NOT calendar year, NOT income tax year).
**Return + payment due:** 21 May (paper lodgement) or 25 June (registered tax agent lodgement).

## The Fear Number

**Gary's worked example: $9,777 FBT on a $50,000 company ute used privately.**

Gary's electrical contracting business (Mitchell Electrical Pty Ltd) provides Gary with a $50,000 GST-inclusive Hilux. He uses it privately on weekends and during holidays. No logbook maintained.

Statutory formula calculation (s.9 FBTAA):
- Base value: $50,000 (GST-inclusive)
- Statutory percentage: 20%
- Days available for private use: 365
- Employee contribution: $0
- Taxable value: $50,000 × 20% × (365/365) − $0 = **$10,000**
- Type 1 gross-up: $10,000 × 2.0802 = $20,802
- FBT payable: $20,802 × 47% = **$9,776.94**

This is the EVERY-YEAR cost. $9,777 in FBT every year the ute is available for private use. Plus PAYG instalments quarterly if FBT > $3,000.

If Gary kept a 12-week logbook proving 80% business use:
- Operating cost method (s.10) would likely produce taxable value ~$2,000-3,000
- FBT payable: ~$1,955-2,932
- Annual savings: ~$6,800-7,800

## Who It Applies To

- All employers providing non-cash benefits to employees or associates of employees (including spouses, family members)
- Sole traders, partnerships, companies, trusts — anyone who is technically an "employer" for FBT purposes
- Includes private company directors providing themselves with cars, paying personal expenses through the company, providing meal entertainment
- Includes "tax-exempt" employers (subject to capping thresholds)
- Does NOT apply to genuine business expenses with no private benefit
- Does NOT apply to salary-sacrificed super contributions (these are concessional contributions, not fringe benefits)
- Does NOT apply to volunteers (no employment relationship)

## The Form

**FBT return:** Annual FBT return lodged on standard ATO form. Item 23 covers fringe benefit categories.
**Form name:** Fringe benefits tax (FBT) return.
**Apply via:** ATO online services for business OR via registered tax agent.
**Required records:** 
- Logbooks (12 consecutive weeks every 5 years for operating cost method)
- Records of all benefits provided (cars, expense reimbursements, entertainment, etc.)
- Employee contributions (if any)
- Calculations of base values, gross-up, taxable values
- RFBA reporting on each employee's payment summary if grossed-up value > $2,000

## The Consequence

If FBT is unpaid and undetected:
- ATO 3-year amendment window after FBT return lodged (or 6 years if non-disclosure, indefinite for fraud)
- Failure-to-lodge penalties: $313 per 28-day period for small entities
- General Interest Charge (GIC) on top of unpaid FBT
- ATO compliance focus areas in 2026: vehicles registered to companies but no FBT return lodged, car private-use undeclared, meal entertainment misstated, EV exemption misuse

If FBT is paid correctly:
- Tax-deductible expense to the employer (s.32 ITAA 1997)
- Reduces company income tax liability
- Net cost: ~50% of FBT payable (47% FBT − 30% deduction-related savings = ~28% net cost on top of grossed-up benefit)

## Key Legal Caveats

1. **No-logbook default:** Without a valid logbook (12 consecutive weeks, valid 5 years), you MUST use the statutory formula method. Operating cost method requires logbook documentation.

2. **EV exemption window:** Battery electric vehicles (BEVs) and hydrogen fuel cell vehicles (FCEVs) first held on or after 1 July 2022, priced at or below the fuel-efficient luxury car tax threshold ($91,387 for 2025-26), are FBT-exempt. **Plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs) LOST the exemption from 1 April 2025** (except for grandfathered pre-existing binding commitments).

3. **Even FBT-exempt vehicles have RFBA reporting:** The grossed-up notional value of an exempt EV is still a Reportable Fringe Benefits Amount on the employee's payment summary if it exceeds $2,000.

4. **Meal entertainment 50/50 split or 12-week register:** Two valid valuation methods. Choice affects taxable value significantly. Method must be elected per FBT year.

5. **Otherwise deductible rule (s.58Y):** If the employee could have claimed the expense as a personal income tax deduction had they paid it themselves, the FBT taxable value is reduced. Common application: work-related cars, professional memberships, work-related self-education.

6. **Car parking trap:** ATO's expanded interpretation of "commercial car parking station" (now includes shopping centre carparks within 1km) increases FBT exposure for many employers. Review proximity carefully.

## The Deadline

**FBT year:** 1 April to 31 March (NOT 1 July to 30 June).

**FBT return + payment:**
- 21 May (if lodging paper return yourself)
- 25 June (if lodging via registered tax agent)
- Quarterly PAYG instalments due 28th of month following each quarter (if previous year's FBT > $3,000): Q1 28 July, Q2 28 October, Q3 28 January, Q4 28 April

**Logbook validity:** 12 consecutive weeks of representative driving, valid for 5 FBT years (must be re-done if usage pattern changes significantly).

**Late lodgement penalty:** $313 per 28-day period (small entity rate). Even a NIL return should be lodged to start the amendment-period clock.

## Common Misconceptions

**MYTH:** "FBT only applies to big companies."
**FACT:** Any employer providing non-cash benefits is in scope. Sole-trader companies and family trusts with director cars are caught daily.

**MYTH:** "If I keep good records, I don't need a logbook."
**FACT:** The 12-week logbook is the SPECIFIC document required to use the operating cost method. Without it, statutory formula is mandatory.

**MYTH:** "The car is registered in the company name so private use doesn't matter."
**FACT:** Registration is irrelevant. What matters is whether the car is "available for private use" — even if not actually driven privately, availability triggers FBT.

**MYTH:** "I salary-sacrifice the car so there's no FBT."
**FACT:** Salary-sacrificed cars are still subject to FBT. The employee contribution (post-tax salary deduction) reduces taxable value but doesn't eliminate it.

**MYTH:** "PHEVs are still FBT-exempt as electric vehicles."
**FACT:** As of 1 April 2025, PHEVs lost the exemption. Only fully battery-electric (BEV) and hydrogen fuel cell vehicles (FCEV) below the luxury car threshold remain exempt.

**MYTH:** "$300 minor benefit means I can give an employee $299 every fortnight tax-free."
**FACT:** The minor benefit exemption requires benefits to be infrequent AND irregular. Repeating benefits don't qualify.
