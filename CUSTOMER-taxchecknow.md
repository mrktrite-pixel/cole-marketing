## THE PSYCHOLOGY

This is what every bee should remember when writing for our audience.

### What They Feel

```
1. CONFUSED
   Tax law is genuinely opaque. It changes constantly.
   Multiple layers (federal, state, ATO/HMRC/IRS rulings,
   private rulings, case law). Even experts disagree.
   
   Our customer doesn't know what they don't know.
   They suspect their accountant might be wrong but
   don't have the vocabulary to challenge them.

2. DESPERATE
   Something has triggered the search.
   Almost always a high-stakes moment:
     "I'm selling my house in 60 days"
     "I just got an ATO letter"
     "My business is being acquired"
     "Mum died and left me her house"
     "I'm moving to the UK in March"
   
   They have a deadline. They feel the pressure.

3. SELF-DOUBTING
   "I'm not a tax expert."
   "I should know this already."
   "Maybe I'm asking a stupid question."
   They feel embarrassed they don't know the rules.
   They appreciate when content respects their intelligence
   without assuming they have professional training.

4. SUSPICIOUS
   They've been burned before:
     ATO disagreed with their accountant's advice
     AI gave them confident but wrong information
     Internet articles contradicted each other
     Their accountant said "it depends" too many times
   
   They distrust authority claims without evidence.
   They want to see legislation references.
   They want SPECIFIC dollar numbers.

5. TIME-POOR
   They want a quick check, not a tax course.
   90 seconds to know if they have a problem.
   Then deeper info if they want it.
   Not 5,000-word whitepapers.

6. MONEY-ANXIOUS
   The fear that drives the click is paying $50k+ they don't owe.
   Or worse: paying $50k they DID owe but didn't know about.
   The fear is asymmetric — they fear loss more than they enjoy gain.
   
   That's why fear-number headlines convert.
```

### What They Want

```
✓ Plain English, not legal jargon
  ("$135,000 withheld at settlement"
  not "Withholding obligation under Subdivision 14-D 
   of Schedule 1 to the Taxation Administration Act 1953")

✓ A specific dollar number
  ("YOU could lose $X,XXX")
  Not "significant tax exposure"

✓ Validation they're not crazy
  ("Yes, this rule IS confusing")
  ("Most accountants don't tell their clients about this")
  ("You're right to be checking")

✓ Action item
  ("Here's what to do today")
  ("File NAT 74883 before settlement")
  ("Ask your conveyancer about FRCGW")

✓ Speed
  Quick answer first.
  More detail if they want it.
  Don't make them earn the answer with a 10-minute video.
```

### What They DON'T Want

```
✗ "Speak to a qualified advisor"
   That's the problem. Their advisor missed it.

✗ Disclaimers buried in legalese
   They want straight answers, then the fine print.

✗ Long-form whitepapers
   They will close the tab.

✗ Sales pitches before getting any value
   They will close the tab faster.

✗ Confident but wrong AI answers
   They've been burned before.

✗ "It depends"
   It always depends. Tell them what it depends ON.
```

## PURCHASE TRIGGERS

What moves them from "concerned" to "paying $X for a product."

```
1. THE FEAR NUMBER LANDING
   "$135,000 at risk on a $900k sale" makes it real.
   Generic "significant exposure" does nothing.
   
2. THE TIME PRESSURE
   "Apply 28 days before settlement" creates urgency.
   No deadline = no purchase.

3. THE SPECIFIC RULE
   Citing legislation (Subdiv 14-D, Section 14-200)
   creates credibility — this isn't general advice.

4. THE PEER VALIDATION
   "Gary lost $XX before knowing this" makes it human.
   Stories convert better than lists.

5. THE EASY OUT
   "Run the check in 90 seconds, free, anonymous"
   removes the barrier.
   Calculator must be fast and concrete.

6. THE PRICE POINT
   $47-$197 for tax check is impulse-purchase territory
   for someone facing $50,000+ exposure.
   Higher prices need higher justification.
```

How to address this customer per platform:

### LinkedIn
```
Tone: Professional but human. Gary tells a story.
Hook: "Gary Mitchell, 64, Bibra Lake. Sold his investment property."
```

```
Tone: Teacher, not lecturer. "Here's what most people miss."
Hook: First 5s = fear number, first 30s = full restate
Length: 8-12 minutes (authority builds with length)
CTA: Calculator at end + newsletter in description
```

### Reddit
```
Tone: Peer who learned this the hard way. Not promotional.
Hook: First sentence = specific number or rule change
Length: 200-400 words (Reddit attention span)
CTA: Optional product mention at end (95/5 rule)
```

### X (Twitter)
```
Tone: Sharp, opinionated, slightly contrarian.
Hook: Tweet 1 stands alone, fear number leads
Length: Thread of 6-10 tweets (Sabrina format)
CTA: Newsletter in bio, calculator in last tweet OR first comment
```

### TikTok / IG Reels / YouTube Shorts
```
Tone: Casual, fast, urgent.
Hook: First frame = fear number, first 3s = stakes
Length: 15-45 seconds
CTA: "Check link in bio" — newsletter primary, calculator secondary
```

```
Tone: Insider letter. "Here's what changed this week."
Hook: Subject line is everything. Test 3 variants.
Length: Medium (500-800 words)
CTA: Calculator link + occasionally referrals/sharing
```
